Meeting: Q1 Planning and Strategy Review

Sarah Chen: Good morning everyone! Thanks for joining the Q1 planning session. We have a lot to cover today, so let's get started. First, quick introductions for our new team members.

David Kim: I want to add some context there. Yes, we lost DataCorp and FinanceHub, but we closed three new enterprise deals in December that will start contributing in Q1. The pipeline is looking healthy at $12M for next quarter.

Sarah Chen: That's fantastic work Lisa. James, what about engineering metrics?

Mike Rodriguez: That's impressive. What was the strategy behind the bug reduction?

James Park: We dedicated one sprint entirely to bug fixes and technical debt. We also implemented a new policy - for every new feature we build, we have to close at least 5 existing bugs. It's been working well.

Sarah Chen: Great initiative. Now let's talk about Q1 priorities. I've been thinking about three main themes: growth, quality, and innovation. Mike, you've been reviewing our roadmap - what are your thoughts?

Third, launching the mobile app beta. And fourth, building out our enterprise features to support the deals David's closing.

Emily Watson: I agree with the onboarding focus. Our data shows that 30% of new users still drop off in the first week, even with Lisa's improvements. If we can get that down to 20%, that's a significant revenue impact given our pricing model.

Lisa Martinez: I have some ideas there. We could add in-app tutorials, personalized setup wizards based on use case, and better integration with popular tools like Slack and Microsoft Teams.

David Kim: The enterprise features are critical from my perspective. I'm in conversations with three Fortune 500 companies right now, and they all need SSO, advanced permissions, and audit logs. Without these, we're leaving money on the table.

James Park: The challenge is resource allocation. The mobile app alone will require two full-time engineers for at least 3 months. API expansion needs another two engineers. Enterprise features need one backend and one frontend engineer. We only have 8 engineers total.

Sarah Chen: Good point. What if we stagger the work? Start API and enterprise features in January since those support David's pipeline. Then shift resources to mobile in February?

Mike Rodriguez: That could work, but I'm concerned about mobile. Our competitors all have mobile apps and we're losing deals because of it. Can we bring in contractors to accelerate?

Emily Watson: Budget-wise, we have room for two contractors in Q1. We budgeted $150K for external resources. If we can find good mobile developers, I'd support that.

James Park: I know a great contracting agency that specializes in React Native. They could handle the mobile UI while our team focuses on the API and backend infrastructure.

Sarah Chen: Alright, let's plan on that. James, can you reach out to the agency and get a proposal by end of week?

James Park: Will do.

Rachel Foster: We're planning a major campaign around the API v2 launch. I'm thinking developer-focused content, webinars, and partnerships with integration platforms like Zapier. We should also invest more in SEO - our organic traffic is only 30% of total traffic right now.

David Kim: The API marketing is crucial. Enterprise buyers want to see a robust ecosystem. If we can show 50+ integrations by end of Q2, that becomes a major selling point.

Sarah Chen: Agreed. Emily, what's the budget look like for marketing in Q1?

Emily Watson: We have $200K allocated for Q1 marketing, split between digital advertising, content creation, and events. Rachel, I'd suggest front-loading the API campaign since that supports both product-led growth and David's enterprise deals.

Sarah Chen: Perfect. Next topic - customer feedback. Emily, you mentioned the retention improvements, but what are customers asking for?

Emily Watson: I've been analyzing support tickets and feature requests. The top three requests are: dark mode with 500+ requests, export functionality with 350+ requests, and team collaboration features with 280+ requests. Interestingly, dark mode is almost entirely from individual users, while export and collaboration are from enterprise customers.

Lisa Martinez: Dark mode is an interesting one. From a design perspective, it's not trivial - we need to ensure accessibility, readability, and maintain our brand identity. But it's definitely doable.

James Park: Yeah, we could do a minimum viable export in Q1. Just CSV format, basic fields. That's probably a 2-week sprint.

Sarah Chen: That's a good compromise. Let's do both - dark mode and basic export in Q1. James, can you work those into the sprint planning?

James Park: Yep, I'll add them to the roadmap.
